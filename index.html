Introduction

Have you ever noticed that songs just feel shorter lately? As a music lover who has noticed this trend I wondered if that was actually measurable, and if the differences in duration were statistically significant. To do this, I built an interactive tool that models the distribution of Billboard Hot 100 song lengths across seven decades, from the 1960s to today, using normal distributions fitted to real data. The results turned out a lot more fascinating than I could imagine. The curves tell a story of how culture, technology, and money shaped music.

Background (click below to expand)

The story of song length is really a story of whatever the dominant monetary format is.

  • 1960s In the 1960s, that was the radio. Stations needed to fit songs between ad breaks and news segments, which created a soft ceiling of around three minutes. A few artists (the Beatles being the most famous example) pushed back against that.
  • 1970s More artists began exploring song duration in the 1970s and 80s. CDs began replacing vinyl. While vinyl had a hard physical limit on how much audio you could press onto a side. CDs did not. The 1980s has the widest standard deviation of any decade in my dataset, meaning the most variety. You had 9 to 5 by Dolly Parton at 2:42 in the same decade as Money for Nothing by Dire Straits at 8:16.
  • 1990s The 1990s were peak creative freedom. Past the days of constraints from vinyl and before the days of constraint from streaming. The average song length peaked at 4:28.
  • 2010s The 2010s brought Spotify, and the incentive structure flipped completely. Artists earn royalties only when a listener stays past 30 seconds, which pushed intros to disappear and hooks to arrive immediately. More plays equals more revenue, so shorter songs meant more loops which meant more money. The data shows this clearly. The 2010s mean dropped to 3:41 and the standard deviation narrowed, meaning the industry was converging on a formula.
  • 2020s Today, in the 2020s, the biggest influence on song length is TikTok and virality. A 15-second clip needs to hook someone instantly. The average is now 3:17, the tightest distribution in the dataset, and songs like Old Town Road (1:53) chart at #1 for 19 weeks. As individuals are beginning to have a shorter attention span for entertainment, combined with the economic incentive structure for artists, the music industry has converged on a generally tight song length.
Dataset

I used Billboard Hot 100 historical chart data (Kaggle, dhruvildave, 1958–2021) to identify the top 10 songs per year. Song names and artists came from that dataset. I then sourced each song's actual duration manually from Spotify and Wikipedia (with help from Claude to do it fast) since the Kaggle dataset does not include durations. For 2022–2025, I used Billboard's published year-end top 10 lists via Wikipedia. The final dataset is 660 songs across 7 decades: 100 per decade for the 1960s through 2010s, and 60 for the 2020s (2020–2025). All μ and σ values in this project are computed directly from those 660 real durations.

Bootstrap p-value test
H0: no difference in mean song length between decades. I use bootstrapping to test this: instead of assuming the data follows any particular shape, the method resamples directly from what we actually observed. It is a way of asking: if there really were no difference between these two eras, how often would we see a gap this large just by chance?

How it works
  1. Combine both decade samples into one pool
  2. Under H0, both decades come from the same distribution — estimate it from the pool
  3. Resample n1 and n2 songs from the pool 3,000 times and compute |mean1 - mean2| each time
  4. p-value : fraction of resamples where the difference is at least as large as the observed difference

Methods

I used two concepts from CS109.

The first is the normal distribution. Song length is the result of many independent choices: how many verses, how long the chorus runs, whether there is a bridge, how long the outro lasts, etc. By the Central Limit Theorem, when you sum many independent random variables, the result converges to a normal distribution regardless of what those individual variables look like. This is why N(μ, σ²) is a natural fit for song lengths. I compute μ and σ directly from the raw durations using the sample mean and sample standard deviation formulas.

The second tool is bootstrapping. Once I had a distribution per decade, I wanted to ask: are two decades actually different, or could the gap in their means just be due to chance? I used a bootstrap p-value test to answer this. The method combines two decade samples (which you can choose on the 'bootstrap p-value' tab of the demo) into one pool, treats that pool as the best estimate of the null distribution (where both decades come from the same population), then resamples from it 3,000 times. Each resample computes the difference in means between two random groups. The p-value is the fraction of resamples where that difference is at least as large as the observed difference. The interactive tool lets you compare any two decades and run the bootstrap yourself. You can watch the null distribution of differences build up in real time and see exactly where the observed gap sits relative to what chance would produce.

Results

The normal distributions over the decades align with the background story. The curves shift right from the 1960s through the 1990s, then drift back left through the 2010s and 2020s. The 2020s curve is the narrowest of any decade, which means there is less variance.

The bootstrap test shows just how significant these results are. Comparing the 1990s (μ = 4:28) to the 2020s (μ = 3:17): the observed difference is 1:11. In 3,000 bootstrap resamples under the null hypothesis, fewer than 0.1% produced a difference that large. The p-value is < 0.001.

Limitations

There are a few big limitations to this project. First, the normal distribution is an approximation. Song lengths cannot go below zero, which means the true distribution is slightly right-skewed. A log-normal would technically fit better, but I chose the normal because it is easier to interpret. Second, this dataset only captures the most popular songs. With thousands of songs coming out per year, the mean and standard deviation may be drastically different if all songs, regardless of success, were included. Third, the independence assumption underlying the CLT argument is not necessarily perfect. For example, a song with a longer verse tends to have a longer chorus, so they are not completely independent.

Future Work

An interesting future extension would be pulling full Spotify catalog data (not just hits) with the Spotify API and comparing the length distribution of charting songs against all released music. That would show whether artists are genuinely releasing shorter songs or whether only shorter songs are becoming popular.
